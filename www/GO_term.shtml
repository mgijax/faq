How do I find all genes with shared annotation to a GO term?

  1. Click here to open the GO Browser in a new window. The GO Browser is located beneath the Search menu, as shown below.

  2. Type the term deacetylation in the text box.

  3. Click the Search button.

  4. The search results list 9 biological processes containing the term deacetylation, and one cellular component having a synonym containing this term. Click the link to the first term histone deacetylation.

  5. The detail page for this term provides a GO ID number, a definition, and a number of paths to this term. These paths demonstrate hierarchically the relationship between the term histone deacetylation, more general high level terms such as histone modification, and more specific terms such as histone deacetylation at centromere.

  6. Click the link to 6 genes, 6 annotations next to the term histone deacetylation [GO:0016575].

  7. The results list genes annotated with this term, including several histone deacetylases and the gene Sirt1. Notice that the Evidence column includes the codes IEA (inferred from electronic annotation), ISS (inferred from sequence similarity), and IDA (inferred from direct assay). The final column, labeled Ref(s), provides links to the references used to make these annotations.

  8. Click the link to J:65145, the final link in the Ref(s) column. This links to the reference used for the Sirt1 annotation.

  9. Back up to the search results and click the link to Sirt1 in the Symbol, Name column.

  10. In the lower half of the Sirt1 Gene Detail page, find the Gene Ontology (GO) classifications section.

  11. Click the link to All GO classifications(19).

  12. On the Gene Ontology Classifications page, click the link to Tabular View to view a table of GO annotations for Sirt1.

  13. Click the link to GO Graph to view three directed acyclic graphs containing all of the 19 terms annotated to Sirt1. These graphs show relationships or paths from the highest level terms molecular function, cellular component and biological process, to the lowest level terms such as "NAD-dependent histone deacetylase activity". Notice that there are two paths between the terms "molecular function" and "NAD-dependent histone deacetylase activity". One path follows a route through the term "hydrolase activity, acting on carbon-nitrogen (but not peptide) bonds" and the second path through "deacetylase activity".
